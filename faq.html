Frequently Asked Questions

What is "Zero Hunger"?
Zero Hunger is Goal 2 of the 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) established by the United Nations in 2015. It aims to end hunger, achieve food security and improved nutrition, and promote What are the main causes of Hunger and Food Waste?
Hunger is primarily caused by:
  • Poverty: Lack of resources to buy or grow food.
  • Conflict: War displaces farmers and destroys infrastructure.
  • Climate Change: Droughts and floods ruin harvests.

Food Waste is caused by:
  • Consumer Habits: Over-buying and confusion over dates.
  • Supply Chain Issues: Poor storage and transport in developing regions.
  • Retail Standards: Rejecting "ugly" but edible produce.
What are the effects of these issues?
The effects are devastating and widespread:
  • Health: Malnutrition stunts growth and weakens immune systems (Hunger).
  • Environment: Wasting food wastes the water and energy used to grow it, and rotting food in landfills creates methane, a potent greenhouse gas (Food Waste).
  • Economy: Billions of dollars are lost annually due to wasted resources and healthcare costs.
Who can help, and what can they do?
Everyone has a role to play!
  • World Leaders: Can implement policies to support small farmers and improve infrastructure.
  • Organizations: The World Food Programme (WFP), FAO, and local food banks provide direct aid and education.
  • You (Individuals): Can reduce personal waste, support local farmers, donate to food banks, and advocate for sustainable policies.
Is there enough food to feed everyone?
Yes, the world produces enough food to feed everyone on the planet. However, one-third of it is lost or wasted, and unequal distribution systems mean that hundreds of millions still suffer from hunger.
What is the difference between "Food Loss" and "Food Waste"?
Food Loss occurs earlier in the supply chain (harvest, storage, transport), often due to lack of technology in developing countries.
Food Waste occurs at the retail and consumer level (shops, restaurants, homes), mostly in developed countries, due to behavior and aesthetic standards.
Does eating local help zero hunger?
Yes! Supporting local farmers diversifies the food supply, boosts the local economy, and reduces the carbon footprint associated with transporting food long distances. It helps create a more resilient food system for your community.
What should I do if food passes its "Best Before" date?
Data shows that 20% of consumer food waste is linked to date labeling confusion. "Best Before" usually relates to quality, not safety. If the packaging is intact and it smells/looks fine, it is often still safe to eat. However, always respect "Use By" or "Expiration" dates on perishables like meat and dairy.
