Food Waste Facts

1/3 of all food prepared for human consumption is lost or wasted.

Where is Food Wasted?

Food loss and waste happen at every stage of the food supply chain. However, in developed countries, a significant portion occurs at the consumption stage (households and proper services).

Did you know?

If food waste were a country, it would be the third-largest emitter of greenhouse gases after China and the USA.

The Food Recovery Hierarchy

The EPA's inverted pyramid showing the most preferred to least preferred methods of handling surplus food.

Source Reduction (Reduce the volume of surplus food generated)
Feed Hungry People (Donate extra food to food banks)
Feed Animals (Divert food scraps to animal feed)
Industrial Uses (Provide waste oils for fuel/energy)
Composting (Create nutrient-rich soil)
Landfill (The last resort)

Composting 101: Turn Waste into Wealth

1. Greens (Nitrogen)

Vegetable scraps, fruit peels, coffee grounds, and fresh grass clippings. These provide moisture and nutrients.

2. Browns (Carbon)

Dry leaves, twigs, paper, cardboard, and sawdust. These provide structure and aeration.

3. Water & Air

Turn your pile regularly to add oxygen and keep it moist like a wrung-out sponge.

5 Ways to Reduce Waste at Home

  • Shop Smart: Make a list and stick to it. Avoid buying bulk perishables unless you have a plan.
  • Store Correctly: Keep potatoes and onions apart. Store herbs in water. Use the crisper drawer effectively.
  • Understand Dates: "Best before" refers to quality, not safety. Trust your senses (smell/look) before tossing.
  • Love Leftovers: dedicate one night a week as "Leftover Night" or repurpose them into soups and stir-frys.
  • First In, First Out (FIFO): Move older products to the front of your fridge/cupboard when you unpack groceries.
